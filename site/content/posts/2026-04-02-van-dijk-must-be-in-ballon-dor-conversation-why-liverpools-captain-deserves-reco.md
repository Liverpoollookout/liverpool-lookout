# Van Dijk Must Be In Ballon d'Or Conversation: Why Liverpool's Captain Deserves Recognition

When Virgil van Dijk lifted his head to survey Anfield following Liverpool's commanding 4-0 Champions League demolition of Galatasaray, you witnessed more than just a defender doing his job. You witnessed the undisputed leader of one of Europe's most formidable defensive units—a player whose influence extends far beyond the traditional metrics that award voters obsess over.

It's time we had an uncomfortable conversation about the Ballon d'Or and its persistent bias against defenders. Specifically, it's time we acknowledged that Virgil van Dijk, at the absolute peak of his powers under Arne Slot's system, deserves serious consideration for the sport's most prestigious individual award.

## The Case For Van Dijk

Let's begin with the obvious: Van Dijk has been immaculate. Since arriving at Anfield, he's transformed Liverpool's defensive identity. This season, playing in a system that demands positional intelligence, physical dominance, and distribution excellence, Van Dijk has been precisely that—excellent across every dimension.

His commanding performance against Galatasaray encapsulated everything that makes him exceptional. Commanding his defensive line with absolute authority, reading danger before it materialised, and initiating Liverpool's attacking transitions with pinpoint passing—this is the complete modern centre-back. Van Dijk doesn't just defend; he orchestrates. Under Slot's philosophy, he's become a quarterback in the truest sense.

The statistics tell part of the story: interceptions, clearances, and aerial dominance place him among Europe's elite. But numbers alone don't capture Van Dijk's true contribution. His mere presence—that Van Dijk Effect, as some have termed it—elevates teammates like Ibrahima Konate, Joe Gomez, and Jarell Quansah. Opponents struggle to create clear chances against Liverpool partly because Van Dijk's positioning makes it borderline impossible.

Moreover, captaincy matters. Van Dijk's leadership has been crucial as Liverpool navigated significant departures—Trent Alexander-Arnold's transfer to Real Madrid, the exits of Joel Matip and Thiago Alcantara, and the entire generation of Henderson, Milner, and Fabinho. In their absence, Van Dijk has been the stabilising force, the voice that ensures standards don't drop.

## The Counter-Argument: Why Voters Will Ignore Him

Of course, the reality is brutal. The Ballon d'Or remains dominated by attackers and attacking midfielders. Mohamed Salah will likely command Liverpool's votes—justifiably so, given his relentless output. Darwin Nunez offers the forward dynamism award voters favour. Even Harvey Elliott, Luis Diaz, or Dominik Szoboszlai possess the attacking flair that captures imaginations.

Defenders face an impossible ceiling. No matter how commanding, how transformative, how essential to team success—voters gravitate towards goals and assists. It's a flaw in the award's construction, not Van Dijk's résumé. We've seen this repeatedly. Defenders must achieve transcendent, once-per-decade brilliance to break through. Van Dijk has done exactly that multiple times, yet the Ballon d'Or has eluded him since 2019.

Additionally, Liverpool's attacking players will fragment the vote. Salah, Nunez, and others will split recognition that might otherwise accumulate around a single player. Meanwhile, Van Dijk's case requires voters to embrace a philosophical position: that defending with excellence matters as much as scoring with prolific consistency.

## The Verdict: Deserve Doesn't Equal Recognition

This is where I land: Virgil van Dijk absolutely deserves serious Ballon d'Or consideration. His performances have been world-class. His influence incalculable. Playing under Arne Slot, he's demonstrated that modern football's greatest defenders must be complete footballers—and Van Dijk exemplifies that standard.

However, I'm realistic. He won't win. The award's architecture doesn't accommodate his brilliance. Football's voting hierarchy places attackers above defenders, and no individual season changes that paradigm, however exceptional.

What matters, ultimately, is what Van Dijk already possesses: universal respect from teammates, opponents, and those who understand the game's true mechanics. When future historians examine Liverpool's 2025-26 campaign, Van Dijk's fingerprints will be everywhere—in clean sheets, in defensive solidity, in the platform he created for attacking colleagues to flourish.

That's a legacy worth more than any trophy cabinet award. Though it shouldn't preclude him from receiving one anyway.
