# Liverpool's Title Credentials – A Realistic Assessment of Slot's Project

Liverpool's 4-0 demolition of Galatasaray at Anfield last week was precisely the sort of clinical, devastating performance that stokes title talk. Watching your side dismantle European opposition with such ease creates an intoxicating sense of possibility – but in this column, I'm obliged to ask the harder question: can Arne Slot's Liverpool genuinely challenge for the Premier League title, or are we indulging in romantic fantasy?

## The Case for Title Contention

Let's begin with the compelling evidence. This Liverpool squad possesses world-class talent at virtually every position. Mohamed Salah remains one of football's most devastating talents, capable of single-handedly winning matches. Virgil van Dijk continues to marshal the defence with commanding authority, whilst Alisson Becker has established himself as amongst Europe's finest goalkeepers. The midfield depth – featuring Ryan Gravenberch, Alexis Mac Allister, Dominik Szoboszlai, and Curtis Jones – compares favourably with any rival.

Darwin Nunez, despite earlier inconsistencies, has matured into a lethal number nine. Luis Diaz and Cody Gakpo provide relentless width and dynamism. The defence, anchored by van Dijk with Ibrahima Konate and Joe Gomez providing capable support, has proven resilient. Arne Slot has inherited a squad that won the Champions League round of 16 convincingly – that's hardly the foundation of a struggling team.

Moreover, the departures of Trent Alexander-Arnold, Joel Matip, and Thiago Alcantara forced necessary renewal. Conor Bradley and Jarell Quansah represent exciting young talent, whilst the recruitment of Federico Chiesa added proven European quality. The 2025-26 season represents Slot's first full campaign with a clearer identity – European success suggests his tactical philosophy is taking root.

## The Counter-Argument: Legitimate Concerns

Yet here lies the sobering reality: Liverpool have won precisely one Premier League title since 2020. Their rivals have invested heavily, developed sophisticated systems, and proven capable of sustaining excellence across entire seasons. The loss of Trent to Real Madrid wasn't merely a personnel change – it represented the departure of their most creative defensive outlet. Nobody adequately replaces that level of contribution.

Mohamed Salah's contract situation remains unresolved as we approach the final eighteen months of his deal. The prospect of his departure at season's end creates strategic uncertainty – can Slot genuinely construct a title-winning team knowing his star player might leave? That's an enormous psychological and practical hurdle.

Furthermore, injury has always haunted this squad. When Salah, van Dijk, or Nunez suffer setbacks, Liverpool's title credentials look distinctly shakier. The depth is good but not exceptional. Curtis Jones, Harvey Elliott, and Wataru Endo provide capable backup, but they're not Champions League-winning alternatives.

The Premier League remains ruthlessly competitive. Manchester City, despite recent wobbles, possess unparalleled consistency. Arsenal have strengthened considerably. Manchester United, Chelsea, and Tottenham all boast significant investment and ambition. Suggesting Liverpool will overcome all these obstacles requires genuine optimism rather than objective analysis.

## The Verdict: Contenders, Not Favourites

Here's my honest assessment: Liverpool are genuine title *contenders*, but calling them favourites would be misleading. They possess the talent, tactical direction, and experience to challenge – absolutely. The Galatasaray performance demonstrated their ceiling. But winning the Premier League demands fortune, consistency, injury-free fortune, and rivals underperforming simultaneously.

Slot's team will likely finish in the top four. They could absolutely finish top two. But suggesting they're the most likely champions requires overlooking significant structural challenges – Salah's situation, the loss of Trent's creative genius, and the sheer depth of their competition.

Liverpool deserve respect as serious contenders. They don't yet deserve status as favourites. That's not pessimism – it's realism. And realism, in football, is often the most honest assessment we can offer.

The title remains possible. But at Anfield, we've learned that possibility and probability are entirely different propositions.
