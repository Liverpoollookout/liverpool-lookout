# Virgil van Dijk Must Be In The Ballon d'Or Conversation: Why Liverpool's Captain Remains Elite

When Virgil van Dijk lifted his head after Liverpool's commanding 4-0 demolition of Galatasaray at Anfield last Wednesday, he embodied something that has become increasingly rare in modern football: a defender who genuinely controls a match through sheer presence and intelligence. Yet somehow, as we enter the latter stages of the 2025-26 season, the discourse around game-changing players continues to revolve almost exclusively around attacking brilliance. It's time to challenge that narrative.

Van Dijk deserves serious consideration for the Ballon d'Or. Not as a romantic gesture towards defending, but as recognition of a player who remains genuinely elite in a role that fundamentally decides matches.

## The Case for Van Dijk

Let's be direct: Liverpool's defensive solidity this season has been the foundation upon which Arne Slot's side has built their campaign. Alisson Becker sits behind one of Europe's most reliable back lines, and the architect of that stability is Van Dijk. His partnership with Ibrahima Konate has been outstanding, but it is the Dutch captain's reading of play, his ability to sweep up danger before it materialises, and his distribution from deep that creates the platform for Liverpool's attacking prowess.

Mohamed Salah's continued brilliance, Darwin Nunez's physical threat, and Luis Diaz's directness have all received—deservedly—considerable praise. But these attacking talents operate with genuine freedom because Van Dijk and his defensive colleagues provide the security they need. In the modern game, where full-backs like Joe Gomez and Conor Bradley are increasingly expected to contribute in attack, it falls to the centre-backs to maintain defensive shape and concentration.

Van Dijk has done this whilst also becoming a ball-progressor of genuine quality. His ability to play out from the back, to find Dominik Szoboszlai or Alexis Mac Allister with precision passes, allows Liverpool to control matches from the foundation upwards. Against Galatasaray, this was evident—the visitors barely threatened because Liverpool's defensive structure was so assured.

Moreover, captaincy matters. Van Dijk's leadership has guided Liverpool through a significant transition period. The departures of Trent Alexander-Arnold, Jordan Henderson, James Milner, and others left genuine voids. Under Van Dijk's stewardship, younger players like Harvey Elliott and Jarell Quansah have developed with confidence. That intangible influence should factor into elite-level individual awards.

## The Counter-Argument

One understands the counterpoint: the Ballon d'Or has, traditionally and increasingly, been awarded to attacking players who score goals and create moments of pure magic. Salah, to use an obvious example, has been Liverpool's most consistently brilliant performer. His goal-scoring and chance creation are quantifiable, visible, and marketable in ways that Van Dijk's interceptions and positional excellence simply are not.

Defenders also face an inherent disadvantage in such voting. A striker scores 25 goals and receives 25 moments of individual credit. A defender makes 25 crucial interventions and often goes unnoticed—indeed, many might argue their job is specifically to *not* be noticed. In a popularity contest dressed up as meritocracy, this is a significant handicap.

Furthermore, there are always other contenders from more attack-minded clubs or leagues that capture attention. The Ballon d'Or has become a competition between elite attacking talents; breaking that mould requires not just excellence but overwhelming, undeniable dominance.

## The Verdict

Van Dijk may not win the Ballon d'Or, and perhaps that's the reality of modern football. But he absolutely deserves to be in the conversation, shortlisted, and recognised as one of the world's elite performers.

The fact that Liverpool, under Slot, is competing seriously across all fronts is inseparable from Van Dijk's excellence. He is not a luxury player; he is fundamental. He is not a supporting character to Salah or Nunez; he is co-lead in every meaningful way.

Football has always needed defenders. The best ones deserve recognition beyond polite acknowledgement. Van Dijk is one of the best. That should count for something.

**Rating: Van Dijk's case for Ballon d'Or consideration is legitimate and deserves serious voter attention.**
