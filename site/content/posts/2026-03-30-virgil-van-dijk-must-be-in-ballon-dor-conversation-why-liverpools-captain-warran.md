# Virgil van Dijk Must Be In Ballon d'Or Conversation: Why Liverpool's Captain Warrants Elite Acclaim

When Virgil van Dijk glided across Anfield last Wednesday to marshal Liverpool's devastating 4-0 demolition of Galatasaray in the Champions League Round of 16, it served as a masterclass in defensive supremacy. Yet as the plaudits predictably flowed towards Mohamed Salah and Darwin Nunez's attacking prowess, one name was conspicuously absent from the global Ballon d'Or discourse: the man who made their brilliance possible.

This needs to change. Van Dijk deserves serious consideration for football's most prestigious individual award—not as a sympathy vote for past glories, but as recognition of present excellence that has fundamentally transformed Liverpool's 2025-26 season under Arne Slot.

## The Case for Van Dijk's Recognition

Let's establish the facts: Liverpool's defensive record this season has been exceptional, with Van Dijk operating at a level that transcends traditional centre-back metrics. Against Galatasaray, he was imperious—reading play with prescient intelligence, positioning himself to suffocate danger before it materialised, and distributing possession with the composure of a playmaker rather than a defender.

But this isn't merely about one performance. Van Dijk's partnership with Ibrahima Konate has created a defensive fortress that has become the foundation upon which Arne Slot's tactical philosophy rests. When you possess a defender of Van Dijk's calibre, your entire team benefits. Full-backs like Conor Bradley and Konstantinos Tsimikas can push higher. The midfield—whether Alexis Mac Allister, Ryan Gravenberch, or Dominik Szoboszlai operate—feels liberated rather than exposed. Salah and Luis Diaz can take calculated risks in attacking areas because they trust implicitly in the defensive security behind them.

This cascading impact rarely features in individual award conversations, yet it should. Van Dijk's influence on Liverpool's attacking potency is as significant as any striker's contribution. Without his commanding presence, would Nunez have the freedom to press aggressively? Would Alisson Becker enjoy such a comfortable season in goal?

Moreover, Van Dijk has carried the additional burden of captaincy whilst navigating a transition season following Trent Alexander-Arnold's departure to Real Madrid. He's done so with characteristic grace and professionalism, embodying the leadership qualities that define truly elite players.

## The Counter-Argument: Defenders Don't Win Ballon d'Ors

Of course, the establishment will counter that defenders simply don't win prestigious individual awards anymore—that the award has evolved into an attacker's prize. The narrative is familiar: Ballon d'Or voters prioritise goalscorers and assist-makers over those who prevent goals.

Fair point. History supports this assertion. Even exceptional defenders rarely feature in the final podium positions. The award's marketing machinery favours attacking flair, and that's unlikely to change.

Furthermore, one could argue that global recognition demands consistent excellence across all competitions against world-class opposition. Whilst Van Dijk has been outstanding domestically, the Champions League stage—traditionally where Ballon d'Or credentials are forged—has only recently presented opportunities for individual standout performances.

## The Verdict: Principle Over Precedent

Yet here's my position: voters should recognise Van Dijk precisely because precedent has been wrong. The Ballon d'Or should reward the best footballer in the world, and that doesn't exclusively mean the most prolific goalscorer. Van Dijk's consistency, intelligence, leadership, and transformative impact on Liverpool's entire system place him among the elite performers of 2025-26.

I'm not suggesting he should finish ahead of Salah or other attacking talents—though one could construct that argument. Rather, he should receive legitimate recognition in a crowded conversation. A top-five finish wouldn't be outrageous. Indeed, it would represent progress towards a more comprehensive assessment of modern football excellence.

Under Arne Slot, Liverpool has discovered a harmony that elevates multiple players. Van Dijk anchors that success. Acknowledging that reality wouldn't devalue the award—it would enhance it.

The greatest teams are built on defensive foundations. Perhaps it's time the Ballon d'Or acknowledged that truth.
